At-sea points to remember

The stuff that's different when the sickbay is moving and the nearest hospital is days away.

Storage, heat & cold chain

  • Sickbay and flats run hot and humid — check that temperature-sensitive stock (insulin, glucagon, Td/Tdap, tetracaine, suppositories) stays in the fridge and log fridge temps daily.
  • Walk the shelves before sailing: pull anything expiring during the deployment window and reorder before you're past resupply range.
  • Heat degrades epinephrine and nitroglycerin faster than the label suggests — inspect ampules for discolouration and rotate crash-bag stock often.

Narcotics & benzos custody

  • Counts, witnessed signatures, and the register — every dose, every wastage, every time, per CAF/RCN controlled-drug policy. No shortcuts on a small ship where everyone knows the sickbay.
  • Reconcile the safe after every use and at every handover; a discrepancy found in port is a lot easier to explain than one found mid-Atlantic.

Seasickness & sedation stack-up

  • Dimenhydrinate, scopolamine, hydroxyzine, benzos, and opioids all sedate — think hard before combining them in someone who still has to climb ladders and stand watch.
  • Prolonged vomiting at sea means dehydration and electrolyte loss — reassess before adding QT-prolonging antiemetics like ondansetron on top of an unknown potassium.
  • Flag sedating meds to the chain of command for watchkeepers, lookouts, and anyone on flight deck or small-boat duty.

Escalation when you're the only provider

  • Know tonight's comms plan for reaching shore-based physician support before you need it — satellite windows and EMCON can close fast.
  • Call early on anything trending toward MEDEVAC: weather, distance, and helo availability shrink the window, and the decision chain (you → command → shore medical) takes time.
  • Anything the guide flags as "physician approval" gets the call made first when time allows — and thorough documentation when it doesn't.

Finite stock, no pharmacy down the street

  • Track running counts on high-burn items (analgesics, Gravol, antibiotics) and reorder ahead of the next RAS or port visit — not when the shelf is empty.
  • Know your alternatives column: if first-line runs out, the guide lists what else on board can do the job.
  • Restock and re-check the crash bag after every use — and drill grabbing it in a seaway, not just alongside.

Heat stress & the engine room

  • Dehydrated stokers and boarding-party members have less renal reserve — be careful with NSAIDs, and push oral rehydration before reaching for ketorolac.
  • Anticholinergics (scopolamine, dimenhydrinate, benztropine) impair sweating — a bad mix with hot machinery spaces and firefighting kit.
